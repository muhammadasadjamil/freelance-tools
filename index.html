The Big Question

🧠 How Much Should I Charge as a Freelancer?

The most common mistake freelancers make is picking a number based on what feels comfortable — or worse, undercutting competitors to win work. Neither approach leads to a sustainable business. Your freelance rate must be mathematically grounded in reality: what you need to earn, what your work actually costs to deliver, and what the market will pay for your specific skills.

The correct starting point is your monthly income goal — not a vague annual figure, but a real monthly take-home number that covers your life. To that you add your business expenses (software, equipment, insurance, internet). Then you gross up both for taxes, because as a freelancer you pay taxes the employer would normally cover. Finally, you divide by your actual billable hours — not every hour you work, but only those you can charge clients for.

This calculator does all of that transparently. It shows you the floor — the rate below which you are losing money — and the recommended rate that accounts for your industry's market value, your experience level, and the purchasing power of your clients' country. Use it as your anchor, then negotiate from confidence instead of guesswork.

Before You Set Your Rate

📌 Key Considerations

Most freelancers overlook these critical factors when setting their rates. Ignoring any one of them means you're either leaving money on the table or quietly going broke while looking busy.

💼
True Hourly Cost
You pay both employee and employer taxes as a freelancer. In the US that's 15.3% SE tax on top of income tax. Most beginners ignore this and effectively pay themselves less than minimum wage after taxes.
⏱️
Non-Billable Time Reality
For every 40 hours you work, only 25–30 are billable. Emails, proposals, revisions, invoicing, skill learning and marketing are unpaid. Your rate must cover all 40 hours from 25 hours of billing.
🌍
Client Geography Premium
US and UK clients pay 2–4× more than South Asian or African clients for identical quality work. Actively targeting premium-market clients is the single biggest income growth lever available to you.
🚀
The Experience Multiplier
A Master-level specialist can charge 3–4× what a beginner charges in the same field. Every year of quality, documented experience is a legitimate justification to raise your rate significantly.
📊
Industry Demand Cycles
AI, cybersecurity and cloud engineering command premium rates due to talent scarcity. Meanwhile, basic content writing and data entry face downward pressure from AI tools. Know where your niche sits.
🛡️
Income Gap Buffer
Freelancers face 2–6 weeks of unpaid downtime per quarter on average. Your rate must include a 20–30% buffer to cover slow months without borrowing from savings.
Math You Can Verify

🧮 Example Rate Calculation

Here is an exact walkthrough using numbers you can verify yourself. This is how every calculation in this tool works internally.

1
Profile: Intermediate Web Developer · US client · $1,000/month income goal · $100/month expenses · 10% income tax · 0% SE tax (non-US example) · 40 billable hrs/week · 48 working weeks/year.
2
Total Monthly Needed: $1,000 (goal) + $100 (expenses) = $1,100/month
3
Gross Up for Tax (10%): $1,100 ÷ (1 − 0.10) = $1,100 ÷ 0.90 = $1,222/month gross needed
4
Billable Hours per Month: 40 hrs/week × (48 weeks ÷ 12 months) = 40 × 4 = 160 billable hours/month
5
Base Rate: $1,222 ÷ 160 hours = $7.64/hour base rate
6
Industry Multiplier (Web Dev = 1.40) × Experience (Intermediate = 1.00): $7.64 × 1.40 × 1.00 = $10.69/hour recommended rate
7
Verification Check: $10.69/hr × 160 hrs = $1,710 gross → after 10% tax = $1,539 → minus $100 expenses = $1,439 take-home (above $1,000 goal ✓)
✓ The base formula rate of $7.64/hr confirms your minimum floor. The industry-adjusted $10.69/hr is your recommended starting rate. Charging $7.64 at 160 billable hours gives $1,222 gross — exactly covering your $1,100 need after 10% tax.
